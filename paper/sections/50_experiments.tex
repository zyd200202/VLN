\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}

We now test the prediction of the diagnosis at scale. We first describe the
paired protocol, then report (i)~the degradation effect that defines the
benchmark, (ii)~two negative results that localize the bottleneck, (iii)~a
mechanism analysis that explains \emph{why} the candidates cap, (iv)~a sweep
showing the bottleneck resists the full intervention space, and (v)~a
methodological warning about sample size.

\subsection{Protocol}
We evaluate on HM3D ObjectNav with the InstructNav backbone
\citep{long2024instructnav}, using an open-source Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct
\citep{wang2024qwen2vl} as the reasoning VLM (deterministic decoding on a
single RTX~5090; no external API), the GLEE open-vocabulary detector
\citep{wu2024glee} for segmentation, and InstructNav's value-map frontier
explorer for goal selection. Choosing an open-weight backbone lets every
frame of every episode be re-run bit-identical from the released seeds and
prompts, which is the reproducibility bar we hold ourselves to for a
negative-result release. Conclusions are drawn at the $N{=}300$ main-split
scale; smaller runs are used only as pilot runs and are never mixed with
$N{=}300$ numbers. Each arm is run against the identical
backbone on the same episodes, so comparisons are paired. We report success rate
(SR) and SPL, the paired per-episode difference $\Delta$SR, McNemar's exact test
on the discordant pairs, and a bootstrap $95\%$ CI over the paired differences
($B{=}5000$). Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} (top) summarizes the instrument. The
primary degradation is low-light at severity~$4$, which
darkens the image while preserving geometry
(Fig.~\ref{fig:corruption-gallery}, bottom). We avoid single-run point estimates
because the clean SR of the backbone alone ranges over $0.27$--$0.47$ across
seeds.

\subsection{The Degradation Effect (Benchmark)}
Structure-preserving degradation produces a large, real drop in navigation
success, which is the effect the benchmark is built to study. Pooling three
seeds ($n{=}90$), low-light lowers SR from $0.433$ ($95\%$ CI $[0.333,0.533]$)
to $0.222$ ($[0.144,0.311]$); the paired drop is $-0.211$ with CI
$[-0.322,-0.100]$, excluding zero. At the $N{=}300$ main split the low-light
baseline SR is $0.37$. Motion-blur at the same severity gives $-0.056$ with CI
$[-0.167,+0.056]$, which does not exclude zero, so we use low-light as the
primary condition and report motion-blur as a non-significant case. The benchmark applies the same protocol across low-light, fog,
motion-blur, and Gaussian noise at multiple severities.

\subsection{Two Negative Results at $N{=}300$}
The diagnosis predicts that appearance-level and where-to-go interventions
should not recover success, and Table~\ref{tab:sweep} confirms this at the
$N{=}300$ main split. Blanket restoration (RES) raises low-light SR from
$0.370$ to $0.410$, a paired $\Delta$SR of $+0.040$ that is not significant
($p{=}0.18$); the per-frame appearance oracle (\textsc{OracleGate}), an
upper bound on any test-time arbitration over appearance, reaches only
$0.427$ ($+0.057$, $p{=}0.04$), statistically indistinguishable from
blanket restoration ($+0.017$ over RES). Reliability-gated where-to-go
reweighting (M1g) also reaches $+0.040$ ($p{=}0.20$). Every candidate
converges to the same non-significant ${\sim}{+}0.04$, and the oracle in
particular leaves no room: no router over restored versus degraded views
can exceed it.

Figure~\ref{fig:case-studies} shows what these aggregates look like at the
episode level, one paired episode per outcome bucket: an oracle
\emph{rescue}, an oracle \emph{break} (greedy per-frame view selection
walks the agent away from a goal RES had reached), and a \emph{tied}
failure in which the baseline parks $0.02$\,m from the goal yet never
commits---reach without success, the signature of the commitment
bottleneck. Figure~\ref{fig:trajectories} shows the same failure modes
spatially: top-down maps of paired episodes in which all three arms start
from the identical pose and the trajectories diverge only through the
intervention.

\subsection{Mechanism: Why the Candidates Cap}
The decomposition $\text{SR}=\text{reach}-\text{commit/stop loss}$ explains
the ceiling: across B0, RES, and M1g, reach (${\sim}0.62$) and commit/stop
loss (${\sim}0.25$) are nearly invariant, so no candidate changes whether
the agent reaches the goal or converts a reach into a stop. The per-episode
logs localize \emph{where} this invariance lives
(Fig.~\ref{fig:decomp}). First, the distribution of the distance at which
the agent \emph{first commits} is unchanged by any intervention---the
B0, RES, and even OracleGate ECDFs coincide, with ${\sim}70\%$ of first
commits firing more than $3$\,m from the goal
(Fig.~\ref{fig:decomp}a). Second, RES shifts the reliability
\emph{distribution} at those commit frames from a median of $0.40$ to
$1.00$, so the frames now look trustworthy, yet the probability that a
commit converts to success moves only $0.42\to0.43$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:decomp}b). Third, the episodes whose outcome does flip
under RES were already at the $1$\,m success margin under B0 (median
rescued episode: $1.1$\,m), so the intervention perturbs threshold
episodes rather than repairing far failures
(Fig.~\ref{fig:decomp}c). Restoring perception without repairing decision
is precisely the $26$-point reach-to-success gap that no candidate
touches, which is exactly the ${\sim}{+}0.04$ ceiling observed in
Table~\ref{tab:sweep}.

\subsection{The Bottleneck Resists the Full Intervention Space}
\label{sec:sweep}
If the loss were a single mislocated module, \emph{some} layer-specific fix
would move it. We therefore sweep six causal families
(Table~\ref{tab:sweep}, visualized in Fig.~\ref{fig:teaser}d): appearance
(restoration and its per-frame oracle
upper bound), where-to-go reweighting, commit-timing revocation, forced
commitment, temporal/multi-view aggregation, and a depth-based multimodal
probe (Sec.~\textit{Probing the Multimodal Direction}). All non-oracle arms
below the main split are scored at a common $N{=}150$ on the same low-light
sev~$4$ seed~$0$ val episodes and paired against the same $N{=}150$
baseline, so all comparisons are exactly on the same episodes. Every
\emph{deployable} family either lands at the same non-significant ${\sim}0$
or \emph{significantly hurts} success: REVOKE, MUAP and DXCV lower paired
SR by $0.12$--$0.17$ at $p{<}0.05$ (DXCV at $p{<}0.001$); the only arm
clearing $p{<}0.05$ with a \emph{positive} $\Delta$SR is the non-deployable
appearance oracle, and even it caps at $+0.057$. Crucially, accumulating
evidence across frames, views, or modalities does not just fail to recover
the decision---it can \emph{amplify} the wrong one, because the degradation
removes the information that distinguishes the goal at the decision point
rather than adding independent noise that averages out.

\subsection{Probing the Multimodal Direction}
\label{sec:road2}
A natural worry is that the bottleneck is specifically an RGB problem and
that adding a degradation-invariant sensing channel would dissolve it. We
test this with a train-free probe, \textsc{DXCV} (Depth Cross-Modal
Verifier): when the VLM proposes \texttt{Flag=True} at a low-reliability
frame, a low-threshold GLEE pass locates the target mask and the depth
sensor must agree---masked depth median in $[0.3,3.5]$\,m and IQR at most
$0.6$\,m; otherwise the commit is vetoed and search resumes. Habitat's
depth channel is rendered from geometry rather than light, so it is
invariant to low-light corruption. Paired against the same $N{=}150$
baseline (Table~\ref{tab:sweep}): $\Delta\text{SR}{=}{-}0.173$ (McNemar
$p{<}0.001$), \emph{a strongly negative effect}. Vetoing geometrically
implausible commits on a frozen agent removes a small number of true
positives along with the false ones; without having \emph{learned} the
joint RGB$+$depth representation the verifier cannot tell them apart, and
the net effect of bolting an extra modality onto a clean-trained policy is
to \emph{lower} SR rather than raise it. A multimodal \emph{remedy}, as
opposed to a multimodal \emph{probe}, therefore requires a detector that
has learned to fuse RGB with depth under degraded RGB---the training-side
fix the Conclusion advocates.

\subsection{Sample Size and Scope}
The per-condition restoration effect is small and sample-sensitive: on
Gaussian noise, an $N{=}40$ pilot yields $\Delta\text{SR}{=}-0.125$
(RES apparently \emph{hurts}), while a matched $N{=}150$ run collapses
to $\Delta\text{SR}{=}+0.000$---the pilot's negative direction does not
survive scale. We therefore
report all conclusions at $N{=}300$, and treat smaller runs as pilot runs
only (Appendix~\ref{app:sample-size}). The main-split numbers use the
InstructNav planner with Qwen2-VL-7B, with low-light as the primary
degradation. The next subsection extends the headline cell to the other
three corruption channels at matched $N{=}150$ paired scale.

\subsection{Cross-Corruption Replication}
\label{sec:cross-corruption}
A reviewer-anticipated concern is that the bottleneck is a low-light
artefact: motion-blur, fog, or noise might preserve enough photometric
evidence that restoration \emph{does} recover success. We test this
directly by holding the InstructNav+Qwen2-VL-7B pipeline bit-identical
across the four channels---mapper, depth sensor, GLEE detector, frontier
planner, prompt, decoding seed, episode list, split, and severity all
frozen---and only swapping the corruption. Table~\ref{tab:cross-corruption}
reports the paired benchmark. No cell reaches significance at
$\alpha{=}0.05$; the sign of $\Delta\text{SR}$ flips between motion-blur
($-0.040$) and the other three channels ($+0.000$--$+0.047$), reproducing
the small-sample sign-flip diagnosis of \emph{Sample Size and Scope}. The
bottleneck is thus not a property of any one corruption but of the
\emph{commitment-on-degraded-input} interface that VLM-driven planners
share.

\paragraph{A first training-side probe.}
If the diagnosis is right, the lever the sweep could not reach should be
reachable by changing what the agent was \emph{trained} on. As a minimal
probe we train a $10^{6}$-parameter residual head adapter on
(clean,\,degraded) SwinL feature pairs from scene-disjoint episodes
($2$k steps; Appendix~\ref{app:road1}). On held-out scenes it moves
degraded features $13.5\%$ closer to their clean counterparts in L1, and
its paired navigation effect ($\Delta\text{SR}{=}{+}0.040$, $N{=}150$,
$p{=}0.377$) already matches the best deployable test-time arm of
Table~\ref{tab:sweep}---while leaving the entire training recipe
untouched. At pilot scale the effect is not statistically resolvable and
we claim no more than direction; we report it as the baseline the
released benchmark is designed to measure.
