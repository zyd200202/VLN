\begin{abstract}
Zero-shot object-goal navigation agents driven by vision--language models lose
much of their success rate once the camera input is corrupted by low light,
haze, or motion, and the default remedy is to restore the image before it
reaches the policy. Through a controlled study on InstructNav in
Habitat--Matterport~3D ObjectNav, with a same-seed paired protocol at
$N{=}300$ and a blind per-step reliability estimator, we show that this
remedy targets the wrong layer. Degradation does not stop the agent from
acting; it corrupts the \emph{quality} of an irreversible commitment: the
agent still reaches within one metre of the goal in $62\%$ of episodes yet
succeeds in only $36\%$, and restoration that visibly repairs the image
moves success by a non-significant $+0.04$. A sweep over six causal families
of test-time interventions---pixel restoration with a per-frame appearance
upper bound, reliability-gated reweighting, commit-timing revocation, forced
commitment, and temporal/multi-view aggregation---finds that every deployable
family lands at ${\sim}0$ or \emph{significantly hurts} success, evidence for
an irreducible commitment error that no test-time channel recovers. The
fingerprint survives a cross-corruption replication (low-light,
motion-blur, fog, Gaussian noise) on the same InstructNav+Qwen2-VL-7B
pipeline, and we document that per-condition restoration effects can flip
sign between $N{=}40$ and $N{=}150$, so under-powered evaluations can
conclude the wrong direction.
These results point the field away from test-time patches on frozen,
clean-trained agents, toward degradation-aware training and learned
multi-modal sensing. We release the benchmark, protocol, and all negative
results.
\end{abstract}
